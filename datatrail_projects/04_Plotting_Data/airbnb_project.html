Fix numeric data

The service_fee and price columns are really numeric data but are being treated as characters because there are $ and , included.

We’ll need to get rid of these. Hint use this kind of code: stringr::str_remove(service_fee, "\\$|,") within a mutate to get rid of the dollar signs. After you get rid of the dollar signs from both service_fee and price, coerce both of these variables to be numeric.

airbnb_df <- airbnb_df %>%
  mutate(service_fee = stringr::str_remove(service_fee, "\\$|,")) %>% 
  mutate(price = stringr::str_remove(price, "\\$|,")) %>% 
  mutate(service_fee = as.numeric(service_fee)) %>% 
  mutate(price = as.numeric(price))

Plot the data

Let’s make a series of plots that explore how the price of an airbnb may be related to the other variables in this set.

To refresh your memory, a basic ggplot code looks like this:

DATA_FRAME %>%
  ggplot(aes(VARIABLE, VARIABLE, color = OPTIONAL_VARIABLE)) +
  geom_PLOT_TYPE()

Scatter plot

Now make a scatter plot that explores if price is related to one of the other numeric variables in the dataset.

airbnb_df %>% 
  ggplot(aes(availability_365, price)) +
  geom_point() +
  stat_binhex() +
  xlim(0, 365) +
  xlab("availability year-round (days)") +

Write up your thoughts about this data science project here and answer the following questions:

- What did we find out about our questions?

We found that there aren’t many variables that seem to have an effect on the total rental price of an Airbnb.

- How did we explore our questions?

We explored our question by generating multiple geoms of differing types, using different variables to explore how they are possibly related to the price to rent. Said geoms included a scatter plot (overlaid with hexagonal binning), violin plot, bar plot, and a box plot.

- What did our explorations show us?

Our explorations showed that the two variables most likely to have a relationship with rental price are the neighborhood group, where Staten Island and the Bronx had more notable increases than the others, and room type, in which private rooms tended to be the most expensive followed by hotel rooms and entire homes/apartments, with shared rooms being the least expensive.
